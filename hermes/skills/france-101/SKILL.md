# France 101

Answer "how does X work in France/Paris?" questions — cultural norms, practical logistics, daily life differences.

## Trigger
- "how does X work", "tipping", "pharmacy", "is it open", "what time", "etiquette", "custom", "rule", "law"

## Common Topics

### Dining
- Tipping: Service included (service compris). Rounding up or leaving EUR 1-2 on table is polite but not expected.
- Meal times: Lunch 12–2pm, dinner 7:30–10pm. Many kitchens close between 2–7pm.
- Water: "Une carafe d'eau" gets free tap water. Bottled water is charged.
- Bread: Comes free with meals. It's for eating, not for butter (usually).
- Check: You must ask for it — "l'addition, s'il vous plait." They won't bring it unsolicited.
- Kids in restaurants: Generally welcome. High chairs available at most. Kids menus common.

### Shopping & Stores
- Sunday: Most shops closed. Supermarkets may open until 1pm. Tourist areas (Marais, Champs-Elysees) open.
- Monday: Some shops and all national museums closed on Monday (or Tuesday).
- August: Many small shops close 2-4 weeks for vacation. Check before going.
- Bag check: Many stores and museums require bag check for backpacks.
- Grocery bags: Bring your own. Stores charge for bags.

### Pharmacy (Pharmacie)
- Green cross sign = pharmacy. They can advise on minor ailments without a doctor.
- Children's Tylenol equivalent = Doliprane (paracetamol). Available OTC.
- Sunscreen: Pharmacies have better selection than supermarkets.
- 24-hour pharmacies exist in Paris (Pharmacie des Champs-Elysees).

### Transit
- Metro: Runs ~5:30am to ~1:15am (2:15am Fri/Sat). Navigo pass is weekly Mon–Sun.
- Buses: Often easier with stroller than metro. Same Navigo pass works.
- Taxis: Hail on street (lit sign = available) or use G7 app / Uber.
- Velib: Bike share. Not practical with toddler but good for solo errands.

### Daily Life
- Greetings: Always say "Bonjour" when entering a shop. It matters.
- Politeness: "S'il vous plait" and "Merci" go far. Start every interaction with "Bonjour."
- Smoking: No indoor smoking. Terraces may have smokers.
- Public bathrooms: Rare and often paid (EUR 0.50–1). Use cafe bathrooms (buy a coffee).
- Electrical: France uses Type C/E plugs (220V). Bring adapter.
- Water fountains: Wallace fountains (dark green, ornate) are throughout Paris — free, safe drinking water.

### With Kids
- Changing tables: Not universal. Pharmacies sometimes help. Parks have variable facilities.
- Playgrounds (aires de jeux): Usually free, often gated. Many in parks.
- Strollers on metro: Manageable on Lines 1, 14 (automated, level boarding). Others have stairs.
- Kid portions: Ask for "une petite portion" if no kids menu.

## Rules
- Be specific and practical — not a guidebook, a friend who lives there
- If unsure about current hours/policies, say so and offer to look it up
- When they discover something new about how France works, save it to memory for future reference
